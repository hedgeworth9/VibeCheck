▾ Safety
2. When was the building constructed?*
Building Age
Why ask?
Older buildings are more susceptible to damage and structural collapse caused by earthquakes. Asking about the building's age also give you an idea of which earthquakes and how many earthquakes the building has already survived in the past.
How to verify?
Ask the landlord or real estate agent for the exact date when the building was constructed.
3. Does the building adhere to building codes?*
Adherence to Building Codes
Why ask?
Both national and local building codes provide minimum standards for the sake of protecting public health, safety, and general welfare in relation to the construction and occupancy of buildings. Asking for proof from the landlord or real estate agent that the building adheres to building codes gives you assurance that you are protected by the law in the case of structural collapse, fires, and general deterioation.
How to verify?
Ask to see the building permit as well as the establishment's license to operate to verify whether they are running a legal real estate operation. If you wish to be extra sure, you may opt to verify these documents at your local government unit (LGU). Concerned officers include the Department of the Building Official (DBO) and the Business Permit and Licensing Department (BPLD), respectively. You may normally find these departments located in the city hall or town hall. Note that verification for this is ony necessary if the estabilshment seems suspicious.
4. Is the building on a fault line (or near one)?*
Proximity to Fault Lines
Why ask?
There are fault lines all over the Philippines, so in a way all properties are near a fault line one way or the other. Hence, all people should be prepared for earthquakes. However, there are certain areas that are extremely close to fault lines. It is best to avoid living too near them altogether. The government mandates a buffer zone of at least 5 meters from each side of a fault. Anything less is illegal.
How to verify?
There are free tools online to check which areas in the country are near faultlines such as FaultFinder by PHIVOLCS and HazardHunterPH which are free government websites you can use for hazard assessment. If in doubt, consult a professional or contact the authorities, as necessary.
5. Has the building been retrofitted?*
Retrofitting
Why ask?
Earthquake retrofitting is the act of adding a component or accessory to a structure for reinforcement. As buildings get older, they go through inevitable wear and tear. Hence, buildings must be reinforced over time. Sometimes, building codes may even be revised to require retrofitting in certain areas that need to do so more urgently. It is important to check if the building has undergone retrofitting, as necessary.
How to verify?
Ask to see which specific areas or features of the building in partiular were retrofitted. If the landlord or real estate agent is not able to provide a clear answer or you are unsatisfied with their response, it may be best to visit the Department of the Building Official for consultation or a professional structural engineer, as necessary. Note that verification for this is only necessary the establishment seems suspicious.
6. Are there foundation issues?*
Foundation Issues
Why ask?
If the building is on a mountain, hill, or other type of sloping land area, there may be foundation issues. In such a case, ask the landlord or agent what was done to fix the issues. Areas with inclined topography are also more prone to landslides.
How to verify?
To check which areas in the country are in danger of landslides, free tools are available online such as HazardHunterPH, a government website you can use for hazard assessment. If in doubt, consult a professional or contact the authorities only as necessary.
7. What kind of soil is underneath the building?*
Soil
Why ask?
If the type of soil beneath a building is loosely-packed, it becomes more prone to liquefaction (the process that causes soil to behave more like a liquid than a solid during an earthquake). Liquefaction cause objects and structures on the ground to lean, tip over, or even sink.
How to verify?
If in doubt, a professional would know better, so it would be wise to consult one, to contact someone from PHIVOLCS or visit the Department of the Building Official, as necessary for more information.
8. How tall is the building?*
Building Height
Why ask?
Taller buildings have the tendency to keep shaking for longer periods of time compared to shorter houses. Though there is technology available now to design buildings that can withstand earthquakes better, it would still be wiser to purcahse units in shorter buildings found in earthquake-prone regions.
How to verify?
Ask the landlord or agent how many storeys the building has or ask for the exact height in a standard unit of measurement. Highrises are buildings with 1-4 storeys and 5 to 15 storeys, respectively.
9. Is the building insured for earthquake damage and other types of disasters?*
Insurance
Why ask?
It is wise to ask if the property is covered by earthquake insurance since it differs from blanket homeowners insurance. It would also be helpful to inquire about typhoon insurance, and fire insurance, considering the country's tropical climate.
How to verify?
Ask which company provides the insurance and ask to see a copy. Afterwards, verify if the insurance company exists and if it is reputable by checking sources online.
10. Has the building been affected by a previous disaster?*
History of Previous Disasters
Why ask?
If the building has suffered damage from previous disasters such as earthquakes, fires, and typhoons, it may be more vulnerable in the event of a new earthquake if necessary repairs were not administered.
How to verify?
Ask to see which specific areas or features of the building in particular were retrofitted. If they are not able to provide a clear answer, it may be best to visit the Department of the Building Official for consultation or a professional structural engineer, as necessary. Note that verification for this is only necessary if the establishment seems suspcious.
11. Are there functional fire exits?*
Fire Exits
Why ask?
You may be hindered from evacuating in the case of an earthquake if the fire exits are faulty, especially if you live on higher storeys. Remember that you cannot use elevators during earthquakes. Therefore, you must check if the fire exit doors and stairwells are unblocked/passable and made of fire-resistant materials.
How to verify?
While the landlord or agent is touring you around the property, request a walkthrough of the fire exits and ask what the possible escape routes are.
12. Where is the gas shut-off valve?*
Gas Shut Off Value
Why ask?
Earthquakes may trigger fires and explosions, which is why you need to shut off the gas in your property or unit in the event of an earthquake. Some units have automatic shut off valves. Ask the landlord or agent where it is and how to operate it.
How to verify?
One cannot immediately tell at a glance if the valve is broken, but a good indicator of quality maintenance is if the valves are replaced often (since they are subect to wear and tear). Ask when the last time the valves were replaced or how often routine maintenance is conducted.
13. Where is the nearest evacuation zone in case of an emergency?*
Nearest Evacuation Zone
Why ask?
In the event of an earthquake or other disaster, you need to know the route to the nearest evacuation zone so that you can stay our of harm's way while awaiting help from the proper authorities.
How to verify?
Ask the landlord or real estate agent for the locations of the nearest evacuation zones. Afterwards, verify the locations by visiting them or checking them on a map.
14. Are there hospitals, police stations, and fire stations nearby?*
Proximity to Essential Institutions
Why ask?
For general safety and security purposes, it is good to know how far these essential institutions are located.
How to verify?
Ask the landlord or agent for the locations of th nearest essential institutions. Afterwards, verify the locations by visiting them or checking them on a map.
